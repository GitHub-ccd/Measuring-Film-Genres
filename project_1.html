Measuring Film Genres

By Oliver Gladfelter

A few months ago, two of my friends got into an argument over whether Avengers: Infinity War was a fantasy or a science-fiction film. Deciding to use a credible source to settle their debate, we turned to IMDb, which conveniently cross-lists the movie under both genres. Thus, the debate evolved (devolved?) into whether the film is more fantasy or more sci-fi.

IMDb will tell you what genre any movie falls under, but not to what extent. That is, we can agree Inception and Star Trek are both sci-fi films, but who’s to say which is more sci-fi? And no, not which film is more futuristic or extraterrestrial - rather, which film more represents science-fiction as a genre, more strongly captures the elements commonly seen throughout the genre. If you were to define “sci-fi” to a young child, which film would you use as an example?

Up until now, there’s been no quantifying movie genre classifications. A movie is either a fantasy, or it isn’t. A movie is either a sci-fi, or it isn’t. To get my friends to shut up about Avengers, I had to change that.

Ultimately, Avengers: Infinity War is much more sci-fi than it is fantasy. It’s also more action than anything. Having settled that debate, I went on to apply my methodology (which you can read more about below) to a total of 2,167 films. Methodology

When reviewing movies on IMDb, users can submit plot keywords. These tags give any movie a high-level description. I scraped the plot keywords and IMDb’s genre classification for any movie with at least 50,000 user reviews and 150 submitted keywords. This resulted in data on 2,167 movies, including only films listed in the action, comedy, drama, fantasy, horror, sci-fi, or thriller IMDb sections.

I then determined how often each keyword was used to describe films in each genre category. (after excluding keywords which were used to describe less than 44 films, which removed highly specific tags, like “klingon” or “hogwarts”). This began to give me a good understanding of the fundamental elements of each genre.

This ultimately does little to capture genres’ defining, unique features and to distinguish them another. Tropes such as death and relationships span across all genres. So while “murder” describes more action films than any other keyword, it’s also common in dramas, horrors, sci-fis, and thrillers. So if trying to get at what makes action unique, we need the keywords used often in action films but not often in other genres.

To determine extent of uniqueness to a genre, I calculated the relative usage of each keyword for each genre. For example, “underwater scene” is used to describe action films 1.69x more often than other genres, sci-fi 1.43x more often, fantasy 1.42x more often, and all other genres less than the average usage. Footnotes

  1. For the purposes of this project, I decided not to consider ‘romance’ as a genre.
    • Mathematically, romance and drama correlate far too strongly with one another. I did initially include romance in the project, and found that the data often failed to distinguish romance and drama. This lead to splitting each other’s tag’s relative usage scores, essentially meaning the two genres cannibalized each other’s genre scores. This also happened between action and adventure when I included adventure as its own genre.
    • Operationally, romance is far more binary than action, fantasy, horror, etc. There is either a love story or there isn’t, making it pretty black and white. Compare this to action: you can have no action in a film, a lot of action, or anything in between. Romance is usually either there or it isn’t, with little room in the middle. Therefore, the romance genre doesn’t lend itself to gradient measurement very well.
